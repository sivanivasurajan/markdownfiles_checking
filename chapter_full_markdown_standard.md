## Summary of Public Comments on the Exposure Draft of the Standards, and GSSB Responses

Date 25 January 2024

Project GRI Topic Standard Project for Biodiversity

Description This document summarizes the significant issues raised by comments received on the GRI Biodiversity Standard exposure draft, during the public comment periods from 5 December 2022 to 28 February 2023, and from 26 July to 10 September 2023. This document outlines the responses from the Global Sustainability Standards Board (GSSB) to the significant issues raised based on discussions and recommendations by the Biodiversity Technical Committee.

The GRI Biodiversity Standard exposure draft was open for public comments from 5 December 2022 to 28 February 2023. Due to the nature of feedback received on Disclosure 304-7 Access and benefit-sharing, and the resulting changes made to this disclosure, the GSSB decided to publish Disclosure 304-7 Access and benefit-sharing for a second public comment period from 26 July to 10 September 2023.

The full set of public comments can be downloaded from the Topic Standard Project for Biodiversity page on the GSSB website.

GRI 101: Biodiversity 2024 can be downloaded here.

This document has been prepared by the GRI Standards Division and is made available to observers at meetings of the Global Sustainability Standards Board (GSSB). It does not represent an official position of the GSSB. Board positions are set out in the GRI Sustainability Reporting Standards. The GSSB is the independent standard setting body of GRI. For more information visit www.globalreporting.org.

Introduction
37

Objectives for revising GRI 304: Biodiversity 2016
38

The project proposal for the review of GRI 304: Biodiversity 2016 was approved by the Global
39

Sustainability Standards Board (GSSB), GRI’s independent standard setting body, at its meeting on
40

29 September 2021. The primary objective of the project was to review the GRI 304 to represent
41

internationally agreed best practice and align with recent developments and the relevant authoritative
42

intergovernmental instruments in the field of biodiversity.
43

The project followed the GSSB Due Process Protocol. A multi-stakeholder technical committee of 18
44

experts representing all five GRI constituencies was appointed by the GSSB in November 2021. The
45

technical committee informed the revision of the Standard by convening throughout 2021-2023 in 10
46

meetings.
47

The following specific objectives were established by the GSSB when commencing the revision of the
48

Standard, and were considered throughout the project:
49

- revising the existing ‘Background context’ information within GRI 304.
50

- developing the topic management disclosures, considering contents in the existing
51

management disclosures section within GRI 304, including:
52

o
developing new requirements, recommendations, and guidance.
53

- ensuring that the revised management disclosures are compatible for organizations to use
54

together with GRI 3: Material Topics 2021.
55

- developing topic disclosures for biodiversity, building on the existing topic disclosures (along
56

with their related reporting requirements, recommendations, and guidance) including:
57

o
revising the existing content;
58

o
developing new disclosures, requirements, recommendations, and guidance to
59

address areas not currently covered by the Standard.
60

- revising and updating the existing Bibliography related to biodiversity.
61

- revising existing definitions in the GRI Standards Glossary and, where applicable, developing
62

new ones.
63

- identifying which GRI Sector Standards content related to biodiversity should be revised to
64

ensure alignment and consistency with the revised Topic Standard for Biodiversity.

Methodology for analyzing comments
100

All comments submitted by respondents were collated by the Standards Division.
101

Each comment was categorized according to its relevance to a specific Standard, section, disclosure,
102

or a group of disclosures in the Biodiversity exposure draft, or as a cross-cutting theme. Each
103

comment was then sub-categorized by the respondent’s:
104

- support for proposed content, based on its clarity, feasibility, and relevance to reporting;
105

- opposition to the proposed content, based on its clarity, feasibility, and relevance to
106

reporting;
107

- concern about the proposed content, based on its clarity, feasibility, and relevance to
108

reporting;
109

- proposals for new content.
110

When a respondent had raised several, different points in one comment, the points were separated
111

into distinct comments.
112

Comments that could not be adequately summarized or interpreted are presented in this summary,
113

where relevant, verbatim inside quotation marks.

116

enable respondents to provide feedback on sections of interest, not all respondents provided

121

responses
122

In line with the GSSB Due Process Protocol, this section summarizes the significant issues raised by
123

respondents, outlines proposed changes to the Biodiversity Standard exposure draft, and explains the
124

reasons why significant changes recommended by respondents were, or were not, accepted by the
125

GSSB.
126

The significant issues identified have been organized into the following sections:
127

- Cross-cutting issues
128

- Issues by disclosure
129

This section includes references to the Biodiversity Standard exposure draft, and the final version of
130

GRI 101: Biodiversity 2024. When referring to the content in the exposure draft, the titles in the
131

exposure draft are used. When referring to the content in the final Biodiversity Standard, the titles in
132

GRI 101: Biodiversity 2024 are used.
133

Cross-cutting issues
134

a) Focus on most significant impacts on biodiversity
135

Many respondents raised concerns about the concept ‘most significant impacts’. These respondents
136

stated that:
137

- focusing on most significant impacts is subjective and may limit transparency in terms of the
138

total footprint of the company;
139

- additional guidance is needed to understand how to prioritize impacts;
140

- organizations with no net loss or net gain commitments need to report on all impacts and,
141

therefore, all operational sites should be considered;
142

- the term ‘material impacts’ should be used instead to align with other standards.
143

One respondent welcomes the focus on the most significant impacts for reporting on the location of
144

sites as reporting on impacts for all sites would be unduly burdensome.
145

GSSB response:
146

- The reference to ‘most significant impacts’ is maintained. Two reporting options are included
147

in requirements 101-5-b and 101-5-d in the final Standard to report the percentage of sites in
148

or near ecologically sensitive areas and the proportion of total high-impact commodities
149

sourced respectively. These options can help provide a high-level understanding of the
150

significance of biodiversity across the organization’s operations and its supply chain.
151

- The requirement to explain how sites and products and services in the supply chain with the
152

most significant impacts on biodiversity have been determined has been separated in a new
153

disclosure (Disclosure 101-4 in the final Standard). This disclosure provides more detailed
154

guidance on the criteria that organizations can consider to identify their most significant
155

impacts on biodiversity, including the direct drivers of biodiversity loss, the proximity to
156

ecologically sensitive areas, and the changes to the state of biodiversity. The guidance also
157

provides more information on the type of data and the tools that can be used.
158

- It has been clarified that it is up to the organization to set the threshold to determine which
159

sites and which products and services in its supply chain have the most significant impacts on
160

biodiversity and that an organization, for example, can determine that all of its sites have the
161

most significant impacts on biodiversity, except for its headquarters.
162

- In addition, the GRI Sector Standards support organizations in identifying topics that are likely
163

to be material for organizations in a sector on the basis of the sector’s most significant
164

impacts (e.g., natural ecosystem conversion) and may include additional sector disclosures
165

and recommendations to report on the topic of biodiversity.’
166

- No changes have been made to the term ‘most significant impacts’ as it is aligned with how
167

impacts are described in GRI 3: Material Topics 2021.
168

b) Reporting on supply chain impacts
169

Many respondents raised that reporting on supply chain impacts, especially beyond the first tier, is
170

challenging. Respondents stated that:
171

- reporting on impacts along the supply chain is important but it is difficult to obtain data from
172

suppliers beyond the first tier and it increases the burden on suppliers to provide the
173

necessary data;
174

- reporting the information on suppliers’ impacts (i.e., information on the condition of
175

ecosystems, and on the ecosystem services and beneficiaries) is challenging and there is no
176

well-established methodology to measure suppliers’ impacts. It is suggested that these
177

requirements should be removed or revised to adopt a qualitative approach;
178

- it is challenging to obtain information on the actions taken by suppliers to manage their
179

impacts.
180

GSSB response:
181

- Reporting on supply chain impacts is maintained in the Standard and requirements have been
182

revised following public feedback.
183

- The reference to ‘suppliers’ operational sites’ has been changed to ‘products and services in
184

its supply chain’ in the final Standard. Instead of reporting information for suppliers’
185

operational sites, the information is now reported for each product and service in the supply
186

chain with the most significant impacts on biodiversity
187

o
Disclosure 101-4 requires information on how products and services in the supply
188

chain with the most significant impacts on biodiversity have been determined.
189

o
Disclosure 101-5 requires information on the countries and jurisdictions of products
190

and services in its supply chain with the most significant impacts on biodiversity.
191

o
Disclosure 101-6 requires information on the direct drivers of biodiversity loss
192

associated with these products and services.
193

- Requirement 304-3-c to report the condition of ecosystems that are or could be affected by
194

suppliers’ activities has been changed to a recommendation in Disclosure 101-7 in the final
195

Standard. Similarly, requirement 304-4-b to report the significant ecosystem services and
196

beneficiaries that are or could be affected by suppliers’ activities has been changed to a
197

recommendation in Disclosure 101-8 in the final Standard.
198

- It has been clarified in the guidance of Disclosure 101-2 in the final Standard that the
199

management of biodiversity impacts only covers actions taken by the organization to manage
200

impacts in its supply chain and does not cover actions taken by the suppliers themselves.
201

c) Site-level reporting
202

Some respondents indicated that reporting site-level information is feasible. A few respondents
203

indicated that site-level information is challenging to report. These respondents stated that it is
204

particularly challenging to identify suppliers’ operational sites.
205

GSSB response:
206

The Biodiversity Standard exposure draft only requires reporting the country or jurisdiction where the
207

suppliers’ activities are located, not the exact location or coordinates of their sites. The reference to
208

‘suppliers’ operational sites’ has been changed to ‘products and services in its supply chain’ in the
209

final Standard. Instead of reporting information for each country or jurisdiction where the suppliers’
210

operational sites are located, the information is now reported for each product and service in the
211

supply chain with the most significant impacts on biodiversity.
212

d) Interoperability with TNFD
213

A few respondents indicated that the use of the Taskforce on Nature-related Financial Disclosures
214

(TNFD) beta framework enables the reporting using the GRI Biodiversity Standard exposure draft. A
215

few respondents indicated that they were not yet able to assess the interoperability between the
216

TNFD framework and the GRI Biodiversity Standard exposure draft. A few respondents indicated that
217

dependencies should also be covered by the GRI Biodiversity Standard. A few respondents
218

welcomed the intent to align to the extent possible.
219

GSSB response:
220

The final TNFD framework was published during the revision of the GRI Biodiversity Standard
221

exposure draft. Efforts were made to ensure alignment to the greatest extent possible. This includes
222

alignment with TNFD’s ecologically sensitive locations and recommended disclosures and metrics. It
223

should be noted, however, that the scope of the TNFD is broader as it covers nature, and not solely
224

biodiversity. No further changes were made to include dependencies, which are not covered by the
225

GRI Standards.
226

e) Positive impacts
227

A few respondents stated that reporting on positive actions and impacts to promote biodiversity
228

should be included.
229

GSSB responses:
230

No further changes were made. Disclosure 304-5 Management of biodiversity-related impacts
231

(Disclosure 101-2 in the final Standard) already enabled organizations to report on additional
232

conservation actions that aim to have a positive impact on biodiversity and are not related to the
233

management of their negative impacts. Disclosure 304-5 also enabled organizations to report on
234

transformative actions that also aim to generate positive impacts on biodiversity.
235

Issues by disclosure
236

Disclosure 304-1 Location of operational sites with the most
237

significant impacts (Disclosures 101-4 and 101-5 in the final
238

Standard)
239

a) Reporting on area of influence
240

A few respondents suggested that the guidance on how to determine an organization’s area of
241

influence is unclear and should include a threshold or a range. One respondent noted that the area of
242

influence may be defined in national legislation. A couple of respondents also noted that further clarity
243

is needed for the term ‘near’.
244

GSSB response:
245

The guidance has been revised. The organization can use a radius if it cannot identify the area
246

affected or potentially affected by its activities. If the organization uses a radius, it should explain this
247

and report the distance of the radius used. No threshold has been included in the guidance. The area
248

of influence varies according to the type of activities conducted and the type of ecosystems and
249

species affected or potentially affected. It is, therefore, not possible to establish a fixed threshold or
250

range for all organizations.
251

b) Area of high biodiversity value is too subjective
252

A few respondents noted that the term ‘area of high biodiversity value’ is unclear. These respondents
253

stated that it is not clear if it refers to economic value or value to conservation and noted that the term
254

should be clearly defined.
255

A couple of respondents suggested changing the term ‘area of high biodiversity value’ to ‘area of high
256

biodiversity importance’ to align with the language in Target 1 of the Global Biodiversity Framework.
257

A few respondents indicated that the criteria for areas of high biodiversity value are unclear. One
258

respondent suggested to ‘separate out methods that have criteria to identify sites of importance for
259

biodiversity (e.g. HCV, Important Plant Areas, IBAs, IMMAs, ISRAs, etc) from corridors, OECMs,
260

critical habitats which are more about ways of managing the sites. A couple of respondents suggested
261

additional categories could be included (e.g., previously legally protected areas, threatened
262

ecosystems).
263

GSSB response:
264

The requirements and guidance of this disclosure have been revised to align with the Taskforce on
265

Nature-related Financial Disclosures (TNFD). ‘Ecologically sensitive area’ replaces ‘area of high
266

biodiversity value’. Ecologically sensitive areas (referred to as ‘ecologically sensitive locations’ or
267

‘sensitive locations’ in the TNFD framework) are defined as areas meeting at least one of the
268

following criteria, in line with the TNFD:
269

- Areas of biodiversity importance;
270

- Areas of high ecosystem integrity;
271

- Areas of rapid decline in ecosystem integrity;
272

- Areas of high physical water risks;
273

- Areas important for the delivery of ecosystem service benefits to stakeholders.
274

c) Data confidentiality
275

A few respondents raised concerns about the disclosure of site coordinates for their operations and
276

their suppliers. These respondents stated that it is confidential or sensitive operational and
277

commercial information, and that it has no environmental value.
278

GSSB response:
279

The requirement to report the coordinates of the organization’s sites has been changed to a reporting
280

option. The Biodiversity Standard exposure draft did not require reporting the coordinates of suppliers’
281

sites, only the country or jurisdiction. Reporting the country or jurisdiction for suppliers is maintained.
282

It is now required to be reported for products and services in the supply chain and no longer for the
283

suppliers’ operational sites.
284

Disclosure 304-2 Direct drivers of biodiversity loss (Disclosure 101-
285

6 in the final Standard)
286

a) Identification and reporting of direct drivers
287

Some respondents raised concerns, or required clarification, on the type of data and tools that can be
288

used to report on the direct drivers of biodiversity loss. These respondents stated that:
289

- additional guidance on how to determine which direct drivers should be reported is needed,
290

suggesting tools such as ENCORE and the Science Based Targets for Nature (SBTN) tools;
291

- the guidance on the type of data that can be used to report information for the supply chain is
292

unclear;
293

- the disclosure should be more specific about which metrics and tools to use to standardize
294

the data reported and to ease comparability. One respondent recommended to report
295

additional quantitative data on the production of goods or services, energy and water
296

consumption, effluents generation, non-GHG atmospheric emissions and solid waste volume,
297

type and destination (at least), separated for each location;
298

- there is a lack of impact measurement standards on the five direct drivers of biodiversity loss
299

and that the quantification of direct drivers will take time and resources and the reliance upon
300

modeled data can lead to greenwashing.
301

GSSB response:
302

- Disclosure 101-4 in the final Standard provides more detailed guidance on the criteria that an
303

organization can consider to identify its most significant impacts on biodiversity, including how
304

it can use the ENCORE and SBTN tools to identify which direct drivers should be reported.
305

- The guidance has been revised to clarify how to report information on the direct drivers for
306

products and services in the supply chain with the most significant impacts on biodiversity
307

(requirements 101-6-e and 101-6-f in the final Standard), including examples of tools that can
308

be used.
309

- Requirements 304-2-d and 304-2-e in the Biodiversity Standard exposure draft (requirements
310

101-6-b and 101-6-c in the final Standard) to report the type and quantity of resources used
311

and the type and quantity of pollutants generated have been maintained. The resources used
312

and the pollutants generated vary according to the type of activities. It is, therefore, not
313

possible to specify the natural resources and pollutants that are relevant for all organizations.
314

Note that ‘resources used’ has been changed to ‘wild species harvested’ in requirement 101-
315

6-b in the final Standard.
316

- The guidance to requirement 304-2-g in the Biodiversity Standard exposure draft
317

(requirement 101-6-f in the final Standard) has been revised to explain that an organization
318

should use primary data. However, if this data is unavailable, an organization can use
319

secondary or modeled data to obtain the required information. Although less accurate,
320

information based on secondary or modeled data can provide useful insights to support an
321

organization’s management of its impacts. A recommendation has been included to report
322

which information draws on primary, secondary, or modeled data and whether the
323

organization plans to improve the accuracy of the data.
324

b) Climate change
325

A few respondents raised concerns about reporting on greenhouse gas emissions. Respondents
326

stated that:
327

- it is not necessary as they are already reported under GRI 305: Emissions 2016;
328

- it may be challenging to report Scope 3 emissions;
329

- it is difficult to link greenhouse gas emissions to specific biodiversity impacts.
330

GSSB response:
331

The requirement to report Scope 1, Scope 2, and Scope 3 greenhouse gas emissions has been
332

removed. The reference to climate change as one of the direct drivers of biodiversity loss remains in
333

the guidance of Disclosure 101-6 in the final Standard. The guidance also explains that an
334

organization can report its greenhouse gas emissions using GRI 305: Emissions 2016.
335

c) Land and sea use change
336
